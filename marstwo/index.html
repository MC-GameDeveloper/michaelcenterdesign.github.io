Mars Two

Anna Garbier & Michael Center

Exploring cultural adaptability through an imagined world of interplanetary human civilization.

From the symbolic structures we use to measure time to the nuanced ways we greet each other, this project explores socially learned constructs. It does so in a dramatic way: by splitting humans between Earth and Mars and asking them to interact.

In this imagined world of interplanetary human civilization, population experts forecast a 50 year limit to human population on Earth. The only currently viable option for sustaining the human species is to fully transition to Mars. A few small, exploratory settlements are already established on Mars, including Mars Two-- the successor to a failed Mars One mission.

By building up this world, we ask:

  • What might this mass interplanetary migration look like?
  • Beyond logistics, what cultural, emotional, psycological challenges might humans face?
  • Which challenges are distinct to interplanetary migration, and which have we seen before?
  • How might we intervene to ease these challenges?

From self to governing rules: meet Deimos and Jane

From individuals to large-scale governing forces: an ecological model of Mars Two

Deimos is a native born Martian. He is 40 (Martian) years old and works as an accountant for the Mars Two organization. He is content with his life, and is happy in his daily and weekly routines. Deimos maintains his civic duties of attending regularly scheduled community council meetings, and participating in weekly resources reclamation to maintain stability and sustainability throughout the Mars colony.

Jane is an Earth native who has been brought to Mars as part of a joint Earth/Mars Two effort to analyzse the potential risks of extreme population growth on Mars and prepare for a new wave of migration. A specialist in resource management, Jane's technical insights are valuable, but do her accolades translate? Where do they fall short?

From these personae emerge a tension. The Mars Two settlement has survived specifically because of its cautious and rigorously planned choices: they are a small settlement, have remained political neutral, and have grown safely within the limits of carefully forecasted resources. Thier culture is one of stability and predictability.

Are they ready to welcome a new wave of migrants? How do they welcome Jane-- the face of this pending affront to their stability and predictability?

How to best intervene

With the use of a Futures Cone we evaluated different approaches to resolving the tension between the two sides.

Preferred

Referencing U.S. legislation from the early 20th century we developed a Resources Reclamation Incentive. Similar to the Agriculture Adjustment Act this incentive would purchase resource surplus at market price, subsidize the resources producers, and aid newcomers in producing their own recources. The intent of this would be to prepare a surplus of food to mitigate a sudden influx of population, while developing a positive feedback loop that means more citizens equals greater resource production.

Probable

Researching how major nations are currently struggling with the refugee crisis we discovered that cultural differences among the population was one of the main sources of tension and hostility. In response to this we developed a Cultural Assimilation program that Earthlings would take part in during the 150-300 day journey from Earth to Mars. This curriculum would be designed to aid in the integration of a new culture and system, and assuage any conflict between the different groups.

Plausible

Emulating New York City's Ellis Island, we thought of a Cultural Exchange Center. This would not be a building for filtering people in and out, but instead would be a place to allow for Martians and Earthlings to come together. There will be medical and governmental facilities to aid in the processing of the newcomers, but the main intention would be a symbol to welcome everyone.

Possible

Going to an extreme we dveloped a system that would be implemented on Earth. This would collect everyone's genetic material and through an algorithm an AI would maintain the perfect balance of population, and would give birth to new people using the given genetic material.

After evaluating each potential intervention, we used the Cultural Assimilation Program to explore our questions.

User testing

Test 1

We initially tested a course syllabus for teaching the ways of Martian life.

The questions we wanted answered were:

  1. What are the benefits/drawbacks of the organization of this syllabus?

  2. How did you originally learn to read a clock and track time?

  3. What do you think is missing from this syllabus?

What we learned:

This experience taught us that the project was still too vague. The feedback ranged from different topics such as safety precautions to necessary technologies.

Moving forward we focused into a specific part of the education process. Allowing us to explore the minutiae of the students needs when making such and extreme transition.

A portion of the Syllabus developed to test the idea of an education system.

Test 2

We developed material to explain how the metric Martian time works.

The questions we wanted answered were:

  1. What parts of these materials are particularly clear, and what do you do you find confusing?

  2. What is your reaction when presented with this information?

  3. How could it better be expressed?

What we learned:

Every test resulted with the user asking the same question, and that was how does this relate to earth time?

Moving forward we learned that it is necessary to ground this lesson in context that the person can relate to.

Third Iteration

Moving Forward

Through our exploration thus far, we have concluded that when taking people out of the context they have always existed it is necessary to offer references to their reality. This is not only to provide a reference point, but also allow for the comfort and familiarity of what is known.

Content
The Mars Two Transit course prepares Earth migrants for a smoother transition to life on Mars.

The course has three units: Logistics, Civics, and History. From the Logistics unit, students will gain an understanding of their new physical environment and how to orient themselves within their new setting. From the Civics unit, students will gain practical knowledge of the legal systems on Mars Two, and will be prepared to participate in civic systems. From the History unit, students will learn the historical context of today’s Mars Two settlement-- from its built infrastructure to its arts and culture.


Structure
The 10-week intensive course takes place aboard the Mars Two Shuttle, in transit from Earth to Mars.


Learning outcomes

  1. Adjust circadian rhythms through structured days and coursework.
  2. Demonstrate proficiency with basic knowledge of Mars Two operations.
  3. Demonstrate proficiency with civic knowledge of Mars Two.
  4. Demonstrate proficiency with the history and traditions of Mars Two.
  5. Demonstrate proficiency with day to day activities within Mars Two facilities.
  6. Demonstrate extensive understanding of Mars Two forms of communication.
