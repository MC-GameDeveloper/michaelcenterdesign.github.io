Mars Two

The Mars Two project began as an inquiry into cultural adaptability by means of a speculative future. It ended as a grounded case study in listening.

Part 1: The Original Inquiry

The original goal of the Mars Two project was to explore humans' ability to adapt to new cultural circumstances. We conducted this thought experiment through an imagined world of interplanetary human civilization, where our job as designers was to aid interplanetary migrants.

In this imagined world, population experts forecast a 50 year limit to human population on Earth. The only viable option for sustaining the human species is to fully transition to Mars. A few small, exploratory settlements are already established on Mars, including Mars Two— the successor to a failed Mars One mission.

By building this world, we presented several questions.

  • What might this mass interplanetary migration look like?
  • Beyond logistics, what cultural, emotional, psycological challenges might humans face?
  • Which challenges are distinct to interplanetary migration, and which have we seen before?
  • How might we intervene to ease these challenges?

From these original questions, we began thinking about the particular challenge of Martian timekeeping and what it would mean for an Earthling to adapt to a new system.

We were not interested in the "information" challenges related to time (e.g. how to track flight patterns or send bits of data accurately). Instead, we were interested in the cultural, emotional, and psychological task of adapting to a new social construct; specifically, one as fundamental as time.

As a means of exploring this question, we began designing materials that would teach the Earth-to-Mars migrants the fundamentals of metric timekeeping as it could exist on Mars.

Part 2: The Lesson

When we began writing our education materials, we did so with a particular hope: that learners would be able to adapt to the new system through immersion; our primary job, we thought, was to explain the system in logical terms that did not depend on specific Earth references.

For example, we presented one Martian day as a full rotation of Mars about its axis or 1,000 'beats'. By design, this explanation made no reference to the more familiar concepts of an Earth day, hour, or second.

From our play testing, one pattern emerged:

Tester: But how long is a day?
Administrator: 1,000 beats.
Tester: Yeah, but how long is that?
Administrator: It's one rotation of the planet.
Tester: Yeah, but...

As adminstrators, we knew what the testers were asking. They wanted to know how long a Martian day was in Earth terms. They wanted to map the length of something they felt (a schoolday, a night's sleep, a lunch break) to a Martian beat. This unit translation was exactly what we'd planned to avoid.

After resisting this question multiple times, it became clear that this question was the important moment. We'd hit an insight, it just wasn't the one we planned to hit. It might have even been the opposite.

In the case of timekeeping, our testing suggests that mental models of time are not only (or even primarily) based on a planetary scale. They appear to be based on daily, sensory experiences. Asking a person to jump out of this footing towards something more abstract is a difficult ask.

If we want to intervene in order to aid cultural adaption, as was our original goal, then this experience shows us that it is worth first understanding the meaningful points of reference from which people come.

Presenting an internally logically sound alternative does not sufficiently avoid the question: 'Yeah, but what does this mean for me?' Nor should that be the goal.
